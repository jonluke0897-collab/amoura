# Product Vision — Amoura

## 1. Vision & Mission

### Vision Statement

A world where trans women date without bracing — where love, play, and community happen in a space designed for them, not around them.

### Mission Statement

Amoura is a trans-first mobile dating app that centers trans women and the T4T community, filters out fetishization by design, and makes trans joy the default rather than the exception.

### Founder's Why

Jon is a cis man who has been attracted to trans women for years and has watched every existing dating app fail both trans women and the respectful men who want to date them. Mainstream apps (Tinder, Hinge, Bumble) treat trans inclusion as an afterthought. Trans-niche apps (Taimi, Transdr) claim inclusion but in practice are dominated by chasers and fetishizers. In both cases, trans women do the emotional and safety labor, and the apps profit off their presence without earning it.

Jon is building Amoura partly because the product should exist, and partly because he wants to leave retail and build something that pays for his life while mattering to a community he cares about. He is a retail worker and musician shipping on nights and weekends. He is not claiming to be a trans woman, and he is not claiming to have that lived experience. What he is claiming is stake — he is authentically part of one of Amoura's audiences (a cis man seeking real partnership with a trans woman) — plus humility, the commitment to build with trans women rather than for them, and hunger, the grit of a solo builder who has to make this work.

The founder narrative is stake + humility + hunger. It needs to be held honestly in every surface of the product and every piece of communication. If Amoura ever starts to feel like a cis man's product with trans users sprinkled on top, the entire strategy has failed.

### Core Values

**Trans women first, every time.** When a product, design, marketing, or moderation decision has to choose between what's best for trans women and what's best for anyone else, trans women win. This shows up in defaults, in copy, in feature priority, and in whose feedback carries the most weight.

**Ship with, not for.** Every significant design decision goes through a paid trans woman advisor before it reaches production. The cap table, the payroll, and the decision rights all reflect this over time. "Feedback sessions" with unpaid users don't count.

**Safety is a feature, not a policy.** If a safety commitment can't be implemented in product mechanics — verification, detection, friction, defaults — it doesn't count. Policy pages are for compliance, not for users.

**Trans joy is a product value.** Warmth, play, and confidence are requirements, not nice-to-haves. A product that is safe but joyless has still failed the mission.

**Move fast, but never at users' expense.** The MVP ships in weeks, not months, because trans women need this now. But shortcuts that compromise safety, verification, or moderation are not acceptable — find a different shortcut.

### Strategic Pillars

1. **The T4T community is the beating heart.** Trans people dating trans people is the cultural default of Amoura. Cis users are welcome guests, not the primary customer. When the two audiences conflict, trans users win.

2. **Conversation before appearance.** Every match starts with context — a prompt, a photo, a piece of the person. Generic swipes and generic "hey"s are not possible by design. This is the primary architectural answer to fetishization.

3. **Respect is earned at the door.** Every user goes through a respect-based onboarding. Cis users go through a more specific flow covering pronouns, consent, and fetishization before they can interact. Nothing about respect is optional.

4. **Radical transparency with the community.** Moderation actions, product decisions, revenue, and roadmap are shared openly with paid advisors and core users. Amoura does not pretend to be neutral; it is a product with a perspective, and that perspective is visible.

### Success Looks Like

Twelve months from launch: 25,000–40,000 active users, 60%+ trans/NB, $15k–$25k MRR, Jon working on Amoura full-time with one or two paid trans advisors on payroll. Product Hunt Product of the Day. Featured in Them, Out, and Autostraddle. Consistent 4.6+ App Store rating with reviews that specifically credit the product for respectful treatment. A few dozen public love-stories of couples who met on Amoura. Retention holding at 30%+ DAU/MAU. The word "Amoura" shows up organically in trans community Discords as a verb ("I met them on Amoura"). More than one VC or strategic acquirer has reached out, and Jon has declined both because the indie economics work.

## 2. User Research

### Primary Persona

Amoura is designed for three overlapping trans-women archetypes. The MVP serves all three through flexible identity fields, intentions, and filters — with Maya as the center of gravity for product decisions, because she represents the largest audience segment.

**Maya, 32 — Austin, TX.** Senior product manager at a mid-size SaaS company. Out for five years, stable in her identity, not questioning anything about who she is. Wakes up, does light skincare, walks the dog, commutes to an office two days a week. Attracted to men. Currently on Hinge with heavy filters and an early-disclosure rule she learned the hard way after a bad date in year two. Tech comfort is high — she knows every dating app exists and has tried most of them. Emotional state around dating is tired: she wants a serious partner, but the routine of screening men for chaser behavior before a first date is exhausting. What would make her switch: a dating app where the screening is done before she ever sees the profile. She will give any new app about two weeks before she deletes it.

**Sarah, 28 — Brooklyn, NY.** Graphic designer at a branding studio. Bi/pan, out for three years, dates trans and cis folks, grew up in queer community spaces. Lives in a Bushwick apartment with two roommates. Has tried HER, Lex, Feeld, Hinge, and Taimi and either deleted them or keeps them as background apps. Values queer community enormously; dating is less about finding *the one* and more about being in the mix. What would make her switch: an app where the community feels queer and trans from the first screen, not a "trans-friendly version of Tinder."

**Alex, 24 — Oakland, CA.** Non-binary trans person, they/them, works at a local nonprofit doing community organizing. Activist, reader, gamer. Explicitly T4T — dates only trans and NB people because they are tired of being someone's learning experience. Small but deeply online circle of friends. Has never found a dating app that feels right; relies on Discord community dating and friend-of-friend intros. What would make them switch: an app where the T4T filter is a first-class feature, not buried under eight layers of mainstream UX.

### Secondary Personas

**Respectful cis men attracted to trans women.** Late 20s to mid 40s, often professionals, have dated trans women before or want to. On mainstream apps they face awkward fumbling — not sure whether or how to express their attraction without coming off as fetishizing. On trans-niche apps they feel surrounded by men who clearly are fetishizing, and some of them are embarrassed by the company. They want to show up as partners. They are welcomed on Amoura as guests, required to complete a specific respect-pledge flow before interacting, and their presence is a necessary part of the product's value for trans women like Maya — but they are not the primary customer.

**Queer cis women (bi, pan, lesbian) attracted to trans women and NB folks.** 20s to 30s, often already in queer community spaces. Wants a queer-first app that doesn't ghettoize trans women. HER is mostly cis lesbians; Lex is text-only. Amoura is a natural fit.

**Trans men and non-binary folks in the T4T core.** Trans men dating trans women, NB folks dating other NB folks, T4T relationships of every configuration. This is the community that gives the app its cultural DNA; their presence creates the environment that makes Maya, Sarah, and Alex feel at home.

### Jobs To Be Done

**Functional:** When I open Amoura, I want to see profiles of people who might actually want to date me respectfully, so I don't waste my evening filtering messages from chasers.

**Functional:** When I build my profile, I want to express my identity and intentions clearly without opening myself up to harassment, so that the right people can find me and the wrong people self-select out.

**Emotional:** When I use this app, I want to feel like I am in a space where I belong — not performing trans, not defending trans, not explaining trans — so that dating can feel like dating instead of identity work.

**Emotional:** When I get a message from someone new, I want to feel curious and hopeful, not braced and suspicious, so that I can show up as a full person in the conversation.

**Social:** When I tell my trans friends I am on this app, I want them to nod knowingly, not wince — because their approval is the fastest way this product grows, and their disapproval is the fastest way it dies.

**Social:** When a cis friend asks about this app, I want to be able to explain it cleanly without hedging, because the framing of the product matters to how I get treated when people hear about it.

### Pain Points

**Fetishization (severity: critical, frequency: daily on existing apps).** Trans women are treated as a kink rather than a person. Messages focus on body parts, there is a presumption of sexual availability, and even "compliments" carry fetishizing subtext. On existing apps, the current response is some combination of heavy filters, disclosure calculus, or simply not using the app. The consequence of not solving this is not just discomfort — it is that trans women exit the app, which ends the product.

**Safety (severity: critical, frequency: monthly or worse).** Harassment, outing to third parties, stalking, and in-person violence. Existing reporting tools are opaque and ineffective; banned users return; verification is cis-biased. Current response: heavy pre-date screening, sharing locations with friends, living with low-grade vigilance.

**Disclosure double-bind (severity: high, frequency: every match).** Disclose early, face harassment or rejection. Don't disclose, be accused of deception — or worse, face violence when someone finds out. There is no clean answer on existing apps. Current response: individual rules of thumb that each user has to build from scratch.

**Dating fatigue (severity: high, frequency: constant).** After years of filtering, screening, and disappointing matches, many trans women have quietly given up on apps. Current response: staying single or relying on friend-of-friend intros.

**Invisibility or tokenization (severity: medium, frequency: whenever using mixed-identity apps).** Trans women either feel invisible on mainstream apps or feel like the "trans section" at trans-niche apps. Neither is a place where love forms naturally.

**Cis partners performing allyship (severity: medium, frequency: common).** Even well-meaning cis partners often arrive fumbling or loudly ally-coded, which is its own kind of exhausting. Current response: a lot of teaching work, or filtering for cis partners who already get it.

### Current Alternatives & Competitive Landscape

**Mainstream dating apps (Tinder, Hinge, Bumble).** What they do well: scale, matching algorithms, familiar UX, enormous user bases. Where they fall short for trans women: cis-default UX with identity fields bolted on, weak identity/pronoun handling, no meaningful moderation of transphobic or fetishizing behavior, verification that struggles with trans photos, reporting that is opaque and often gendered against the reporter. Switching cost: already low — most trans women have already tried them and given up.

**Trans-niche apps (Taimi, Transdr).** What they do well: at least they exist and say the word "trans." Where they fall short: the user base skews heavily toward cis men seeking fetish content; trans women experience the same fetishization dynamic as on mainstream apps, just concentrated; UX and product quality trail the mainstream apps significantly; reviews from trans women are poor. Switching cost: near zero — Amoura's hardest challenge is overcoming "I've seen this movie before."

**HER.** What it does well: a genuinely queer-first app with a loyal community, primarily queer women and NB folks. Where it falls short for trans women specifically: the center of gravity is cis queer women, and trans women have reported feeling like a secondary audience. Matching with cis men is not part of the proposition. Switching cost: for trans women seeking to date men, HER doesn't solve the problem; for trans women dating women, HER is a reasonable alternative and Amoura has to win on trans-centeredness.

**Lex.** What it does well: text-based queer personals with a distinct, intentional vibe. Where it falls short: niche, small user base, text-heavy format not suited to broader dating. Switching cost: Lex users who also want a photo-based dating product will use both.

**Feeld.** What it does well: explicitly kink- and poly-friendly, welcomes non-conventional configurations. Where it falls short for this audience: the default dynamic is explicitly sexual, which can reproduce or amplify the fetishization dynamic Amoura is designed to counter. Switching cost: some overlap in user base, but Amoura is positioned at a different vibe.

**Off-app: Discords, meetups, friend-of-friend, nothing.** Many trans women have exited the app economy entirely. These alternatives are emotional defaults, not product defaults — which means Amoura has to earn its place by being demonstrably better than giving up.

### Key Assumptions to Validate

1. **We assume trans women will try a new dating app despite app fatigue, because word-of-mouth from trusted trans peers can break through the fatigue.** To validate: the community beta phase has to show organic pull — trans women inviting other trans women — within the first 100 users. If it doesn't, GTM is broken and the product needs a softer re-launch.

2. **We assume respectful cis men are a meaningful user segment who will accept guest-status framing and respect-pledge onboarding.** To validate: track onboarding completion rate for cis users specifically. If more than 40% drop off at the respect pledge, the friction is too high or the framing is wrong.

3. **We assume a Hinge-style prompt-and-match format will filter out generic chaser behavior effectively.** To validate: audit the first 500 conversation-opening messages for fetish patterns. Target: under 5% flagged for fetishization. If higher, the prompt set is not doing the work and the model needs revisiting.

4. **We assume trans women will pay $14.99–19.99/month for a premium tier once the free product has demonstrated value.** To validate: watch subscription conversion in months 2–3 post-launch. Target: 4% free-to-paid conversion. If under 2%, the paid features are not differentiated enough from free.

5. **We assume a solo founder can ship an MVP that meets the safety bar trans women require.** To validate: gate the public launch on a red-team safety review by paid trans women advisors. If the review surfaces critical gaps, delay launch rather than ship compromised.

6. **We assume the queer press (Them, Out, Autostraddle) will cover the launch story, given the founder-market-fit tension (a cis man building a trans-first app).** To validate: pitch one outlet in advance of launch with the founder-market honesty story. If none will cover it, PR strategy needs a Plan B (Substack newsletters, trans community leaders, Product Hunt).

7. **We assume the "built with, not for" commitment can be maintained as the company grows, including the advisor budget.** To validate: set the paid advisor budget as a fixed percentage of revenue (e.g. 8%) in month one and protect it. If runway pressure pushes the number below that floor, the mission is drifting.

8. **We assume T4T users and trans-women-dating-cis-men users can share one product without creating two broken experiences.** To validate: segment retention and satisfaction by user type monthly. If one segment is thriving and another is churning, rethink the filter/intentions design.

### User Journey Map

**Awareness.** Maya sees a post from a trans creator she follows on TikTok about a new dating app called Amoura. The creator is candid — this is a new thing, it's cis-founded, but the founder is listening, and there are paid trans advisors on the team. Maya screenshots the post and saves it for later but doesn't download yet. Emotion: curious but guarded.

**Consideration.** Over the next week, two more trans creators she follows mention Amoura. One has a referral code. Maya's trans friend group chat starts talking about it: "so, is this one going to be different or not?" Consensus in the chat is: worth trying, low stakes. Maya downloads. Emotion: cautiously hopeful.

**First use.** Onboarding opens with warm copy and a short animated scroll about what Amoura is. Identity fields come early and are thoughtful: pronouns, gender, orientation, T4T preference, intentions. Photo upload + ID verification. Prompts that feel witty and inviting. She builds a profile in 12 minutes. At the end, she hits a respect pledge screen that she mostly skims but notices is specifically worded for cis users — for her it's a lighter acknowledgment. Emotion: this feels different.

**Magic moment.** Within 48 hours, a match: someone who commented thoughtfully on her "a shower thought" prompt about the sonic difference between indie rock pre- and post-Vampire Weekend. The message is two sentences, funny, curious, not body-focused. Not a "hey." She replies. Conversation continues for three days and turns into a coffee date. Emotion: oh — this is what it's supposed to feel like.

**Habit formation.** Over the next three weeks, Maya opens Amoura three to four times a day. She likes six or seven profiles a week. She has one good conversation active at a time. The app doesn't feel like a grind; it feels like checking in with a community. Emotion: relaxed.

**Advocacy.** At week five, Maya posts a tweet: "finally a dating app that doesn't make me want to delete it within three days." Two friends download because of the tweet. One of them matches within 24 hours. Amoura grows one person at a time.

## 3. Product Strategy

### Product Principles

**Identity is the default, not a feature.** Pronouns, gender, and orientation are first-class fields, shown prominently, and edited easily. The app never asks a user to justify or explain their identity; it just assumes it and respects it.

**Context before conversation.** Every message must be attached to a specific prompt, photo, or profile detail. Generic "hey"s are architecturally impossible. This is the primary defense against fetish-messaging.

**Respect is gated, not suggested.** Users cannot skip respect onboarding. Cis users get a longer, more specific flow. Completion is required for interaction, not for installation.

**Moderation is visible.** When a user reports, they see the outcome. When a user is moderated, they see why. Opaque moderation is a policy problem for existing apps; transparent moderation is a product feature for Amoura.

**Delight is load-bearing.** Amoura is warm and radiant because the trans women who use it need joy, not just safety. A safe but joyless product is still broken.

**Small before scaled.** Amoura earns density in its niche before expanding. It would rather have 5,000 delighted trans women than 50,000 distracted users of every kind.

### Market Differentiation

Amoura occupies a position that no existing app fills well. Mainstream dating apps (Tinder, Hinge, Bumble) treat trans inclusion as an afterthought — the product architecture is built for cis defaults and trans identity is a pronoun dropdown at best. Trans-niche apps (Taimi, Transdr) correctly identify that trans users are underserved but fail at the next step: they attract the cis-man-seeking-fetish-content demographic at numerical dominance, which recreates the fetishization dynamic in concentrated form. Trans women end up feeling worse on "trans-friendly" apps than on mainstream ones, which is why those apps are rated so poorly.

Amoura's differentiation stacks three legs. **Trans-first by design** means that every screen, default, and algorithm assumes a trans user is the default user, with cis users accommodated around that frame. This shows up in identity fields, in prompt wording, in onboarding sequences, in moderation defaults. **Built with, not for** means paid trans advisors are load-bearing in the product development process — not token consultants but material decision-makers with budget. This shows up in the cap table, in payroll, in release gates, and in marketing. **Architecturally hostile to fetishization** means the product's mechanics — conversation-first messaging, prompt-led opens, respect onboarding, verification, detection — make chaser behavior a bad fit rather than just a policy violation. This is defensible because it is not a feature a competitor can add in a sprint; it is a systems-level commitment that shows up across the entire product.

### Magic Moment Design

The magic moment is layered by user type. For a trans woman like Maya, it is the first prompt-based conversation: a match comments thoughtfully on her shower-thought prompt, and she realizes the message isn't about her body. For a trans person in the core audience, it is the scroll that feels like home — opening the app and seeing that the majority of profiles are trans and openly so. For a cis user, it is the respect pledge — the first time a dating app has treated their presence as something to be earned rather than assumed.

For the trans-woman magic moment to happen reliably, three things must be true in the product. First, prompts have to be written well enough that people naturally engage with them as content, not as a formality. Second, the like-and-comment mechanic has to be the only way to start a conversation, so generic messages cannot sneak in. Third, there have to be enough other users on the app within a match's geographic window to produce at least one real prompt comment in the user's first 48 hours. This third requirement is the critical path: it is a density problem, and it is solved by the community beta + build-in-public GTM, not by product alone.

For the scroll-that-feels-like-home magic moment to happen reliably, the first 100 users in any given city need to be disproportionately trans. The MVP includes city-gated beta invites to ensure density — a user in Austin does not unlock the app fully until there are 50+ active users in Austin. This is friction, but it is the friction that makes the moment real.

For the respect-pledge magic moment to happen reliably, the pledge copy has to be specific, warm, and adult — it should feel like an invitation into a space, not a compliance form. A cis user who skims past the pledge without feeling anything is a failure of copy, not of mechanics.

All three magic moments are achievable in the Balanced MVP scope (8–10 weeks). None require features beyond the MVP.

### MVP Definition — In Scope

**Identity-first profile builder.** Pronouns, gender identity, orientation, and T4T toggle as first-class fields. Intention field (hookup / dating / serious / friendship). Photo upload (up to six). Short bio + up to three prompt answers. Done when a user can create a profile in under 15 minutes.

**Prompt-and-match core.** A curated set of ~30 prompts, with 3–5 rotations per week. Users answer three prompts on their profile. To open a conversation, you like a specific prompt, photo, or piece of the profile and leave a comment. Liking without a comment is not allowed. Done when a user can send a conversation-opening like-with-comment in under 30 seconds.

**Respect-pledge onboarding.** A mandatory onboarding flow that introduces Amoura's values, covers pronouns and consent, and ends in a pledge the user must agree to. Cis users (identified at signup) get an extended flow covering fetishization and guest-framing specifically. Completion is required before interaction. Done when completion rate is tracked and 95% of users finish it in under 4 minutes.

**Photo and ID verification.** Selfie-to-photo verification at onboarding. Optional ID verification with a "verified ID" badge. Done when the verification service is integrated, 90%+ of users complete photo verification, and false-positive rejections for trans users are below 5%.

**Bad-actor pattern detection and block/report tooling.** Block from any screen (one tap). Report with predefined categories including fetishization, transphobia, unwanted sexual content, harassment, and safety concern. Blocked users cannot create new accounts on the same device/number combination without re-verification. Pattern detection surfaces accounts that accumulate reports from multiple trans women for review. Done when every reported user receives a moderation decision within 48 hours in MVP (this is a staffing commitment, not just a product commitment).

**Audio prompts.** Users can record a 30-second audio clip as one of their prompt answers. Audio is transcribed for accessibility and search. Done when upload-to-playback latency is under 3 seconds and audio plays inline without leaving the profile view.

**Real-time messaging.** Text messaging with read receipts. No photo sharing in initial messages (unlocks after 5 back-and-forth messages). No voice/video calls in MVP. Done when the chat is reactive (Convex-native) and messages arrive in under 500ms.

**Freemium tier.** Free: unlimited profile views, one like per day, send and receive messages. Paid ($14.99/mo): unlimited likes, see who liked you, advanced filters (T4T-only, intentions, distance), priority support. Done when RevenueCat is wired up and the paywall converts cleanly.

### Explicitly Out of Scope

**Video calling.** Tempting because it reduces catfishing risk. Deferred because it requires non-trivial engineering, moderation tooling, and privacy review. Reconsider in month 6.

**Community/group feeds.** Tempting because it would differentiate further and create retention outside of matching. Deferred because it is a second product — build dating right first, then layer. Reconsider in month 9.

**Events / IRL meetups.** Tempting because it creates network density. Deferred because it requires legal and safety infrastructure that a solo founder cannot build safely. Reconsider after $20k MRR and at least one hired advisor on payroll.

**Location-sharing for dates.** Tempting as a safety feature. Deferred because it is hard to build well — users should use existing tools (iMessage, Find My, Life360) that they are already using. Revisit if advisors say otherwise.

**AI matchmaker / "pick for me" mode.** Tempting as a premium feature. Deferred because it is a huge rabbit hole, and the prompt-and-match format already handles the matching problem well. Reconsider at month 12.

**Swipe UI / Tinder-style stacks.** Tempting because users expect it. Deferred permanently — the swipe UX is the thing we are explicitly rejecting. Liking happens from within profiles, not from a stack. This is a product-identity decision.

**Non-English support.** Deferred to year 2. The MVP is US-English only. Spanish is the first expansion.

**Android-only beta.** Deferred — iOS and Android ship simultaneously via Expo. Splitting betas splits the community and the data.

### Feature Priority (MoSCoW)

**Must Have (in MVP):** Identity-first profile builder, prompt-and-match core, respect-pledge onboarding, photo verification, block/report tooling, real-time messaging, freemium tier with RevenueCat.

**Should Have (MVP if time permits, otherwise v1.1):** ID verification with badge, audio prompts, bad-actor pattern detection, intentions field and filters.

**Could Have (v1.2–v2, within first 6 months):** Verified ally badges for cis users who complete deeper training, curated prompts from trans community leaders, "safety pulse" in-app check-in during dates, light community feed.

**Won't Have (this time):** Video calls, events, location sharing, AI matchmaker, swipe UI, non-English, Android-only launch.

### Core User Flows

**1. Sign up and first profile build (new trans user).** Trigger: user opens the app after download. Steps: welcome screen → identity fields (pronouns, gender, orientation, T4T toggle) → photo upload (6 slots) → photo verification → three prompt answers (guided by example prompts) → intentions field → short bio → respect pledge (short, warm version for trans users) → notification opt-in → home. Outcome: a complete profile. Success criteria: 85% of starters complete a profile on first session; median time to profile is under 15 minutes.

**2. First conversation (existing user).** Trigger: user opens the app and browses profiles. Steps: scroll / browse profiles filtered by intentions + distance → open a profile → read prompts and view photos → tap "like" on a specific prompt → compose a comment (required) → send. Outcome: the like lands on the other user's inbox; a chat opens if they like back. Success criteria: 70% of daily actives open at least one profile; 40% send at least one like per session; 25% of like-with-comments receive a response.

**3. Cis user onboarding.** Trigger: user identifies as cis at signup. Steps: welcome → identity fields → respect pledge (extended: pronouns, consent, what fetishization looks like, guest-framing, consequences of violation) → photo upload → verification → prompt answers → intentions → home. Outcome: a cis user who has been explicitly prepared to show up well. Success criteria: 70% completion through the extended pledge; reported rate of fetishization behavior is less than half of the industry baseline within 90 days of launch.

### Success Metrics

**Primary metric:** Weekly active trans/NB users (WAU-TNB). The product only works if trans women and the T4T core are showing up week over week. Everything else is downstream.

**Secondary metrics:** Match-to-conversation rate (did a like-with-comment lead to a reply?), conversation-to-date rate (did a conversation lead to a meet-up? self-reported weekly), week-4 retention of trans users specifically, free-to-paid conversion rate.

**Leading indicators:** Respect-pledge completion rate, reports per 1,000 conversations, percent of conversations opened with a prompt comment (target: 100% by design), average photo-verification completion time, number of unique prompts engaged with per user.

**Thresholds.** Good: 30% week-4 retention of trans users, 4% free-to-paid conversion, under 10 reports per 1,000 conversations, 4.5+ App Store rating. Great: 45% week-4 retention, 7% conversion, under 3 reports per 1,000 conversations, 4.7+ App Store rating with reviews that specifically name safety and respect.

### Risks

**Risk: density failure in launch cities.** Likelihood: high. Impact: critical. Mitigation: city-gated rollout; hold back public launch in any city until there are 50+ trans/NB active users there. Lean on community beta distribution rather than broad marketing.

**Risk: cis men numerical dominance.** Likelihood: medium-high. Impact: critical (recreates the fetishization dynamic Amoura is designed to prevent). Mitigation: onboarding friction for cis users, hard caps on cis-user-to-trans-user ratios per city if necessary, active moderation, and the option to flip on "T4T only" mode for any user.

**Risk: founder-market-fit public backlash.** Likelihood: medium. Impact: high. Mitigation: the "cis founder building trans-first" framing has to be named directly in every piece of marketing. Advisor relationships have to be real and visible. Silence or spin will invite a justified backlash.

**Risk: App Store approval friction.** Likelihood: medium. Impact: high (blocks launch). Mitigation: budget 3–4 weeks for App Store review. Dating apps get scrutiny, especially ones with strong identity claims. Have a legal review done before submission.

**Risk: moderation workload exceeds a solo founder's capacity.** Likelihood: medium-high. Impact: critical. Mitigation: hire one part-time moderator from the advisor pool before public launch. Invest in automated pattern detection to reduce human-review volume. Cap new city launches if moderation SLA slips.

**Risk: subscription conversion below 4%.** Likelihood: medium. Impact: high (kills the "quit retail in 6 months" timeline). Mitigation: the paid tier features are tuned specifically for what trans women want — advanced filters, see-who-liked-you, priority support. If conversion underperforms, the paid tier gets renegotiated with advisors before promotional discounts.

**Risk: a safety incident attributed to Amoura.** Likelihood: low-medium. Impact: critical. Mitigation: safety is not a ship-later feature; verification and reporting are day-one requirements. Have a crisis-response protocol written and ready at launch. Never trade user safety for growth numbers.

**Risk: competitor copies the model.** Likelihood: low in year 1, medium in year 2. Impact: medium. Mitigation: the "built with, not for" commitment is expensive and difficult to fake, which is part of the moat. Keep trans advisors on payroll and let that be visible.

## 4. Brand Strategy

### Positioning Statement

For trans women and the T4T community who are tired of dating apps that treat them as an afterthought or a fetish, Amoura is the dating app that centers trans women in a queer-affirming community. Unlike mainstream apps where trans identity is a pronoun field, or trans-niche apps dominated by chasers, Amoura is built with trans women from day one and designed to architecturally filter fetishization out of the product.

### Brand Personality

If Amoura were a person, she is a close trans friend who's been through it. Probably in her late twenties or early thirties, works in something creative or community-adjacent, has an exceptional music taste and a slightly messy apartment full of plants and books. She remembers your birthday. She is fiercely protective of her people and completely warm to the ones who deserve it. Her humor is quick but kind. She doesn't suffer chasers or performative allies, and she'll tell you directly when she's not having it — but she'd also be the first to text you after a bad date to make sure you got home.

She dresses in warm neutrals with one good piece of vintage jewelry. She would not wear a rainbow shirt on Pride; she'd wear a vintage linen shirt and show up to march anyway. She never says "leverage," "synergy," or "empower." She says "I've got you," "come in," and "that was not it."

### Brand Anti-Patterns

**Never rainbow-wash.** Do not put a rainbow on a button, a hero image, or a launch graphic just because it's Pride. Pride messaging should be specific to what Amoura actually does, not aesthetic allyship.

**Never use clinical or medicalized language about trans identity.** Do not write "pre-op / post-op," "biologically male," "assigned gender," or any framing that reduces trans identity to a medical category. Never include a "body type" field that becomes a de facto anatomy field.

**Never use stock photography of performatively diverse models.** All imagery comes from the community — commissioned photography of real users (with consent and compensation), illustration, or typographic design. Never a Getty Images "happy diverse couple."

**Never position cis users as heroes.** Do not have a "meet our allies" spotlight. Do not celebrate cis men for showing up respectfully — that is the bare minimum, not a virtue. Never make an advertising campaign whose emotional subject is a cis person.

**Never use body-focused imagery or fetish-adjacent language.** No close-ups of body parts in marketing. No "seductive," "curvy," "smooth," or similar aesthetic language in copy anywhere. Ever.

**Never "love wins" saccharine.** Avoid Hallmark-card framings of love, especially trans love. The brand voice is warm and real, not performatively tender.

**Never lean on trans flag colors as a design crutch.** The blue/pink/white palette is not Amoura's default. Reference the flag subtly (a micro-gradient, a hidden pride easter egg) at most. The product palette lives in the soft-glow family described in the design direction.

**Never treat trans identity as a product feature.** Don't say "Amoura supports trans users" in any marketing copy. Trans identity is the default, not a capability. Everything about the product assumes that.

**Never let cis voices be the loudest.** The About page, the press kit, the marketing, and the community spotlight all feature trans women first. If a cis founder has to speak publicly (and he will), it is with named trans advisors alongside.

**Never corporate.** No "leverage," "synergy," "empower," "seamless," "curated," "journey," "solution." No business-speak in user-facing copy, marketing, or community posts.

## 5. Design Direction

### Design Philosophy

**Warm over clever.** Amoura's design chooses warmth over visual wit. If a choice is between a clever flourish and a warm, readable, accessible version, warmth wins every time.

**Identity fields with dignity.** Every place where a user enters identity information — pronouns, gender, orientation, T4T — gets first-class typographic treatment. These are not small fields tucked into a settings page; they are displayed with care, like the most important thing on the screen, because they are.

**Space is a feature.** The UI breathes. Profiles are not packed edge-to-edge; they have room. This is about dignity, not about aesthetic minimalism — cramped identity UI feels like a DMV form, and Amoura is not a DMV form.

**Delight in the seams.** Micro-animations, haptic feedback, and small typographic moments exist to make the experience feel alive. They do not exist on hero screens (which stay calm) but live in the transitions, the confirmations, the "you matched" moments.

### Visual Mood

Soft glow. The overall aesthetic pulls from early-golden-hour natural light, a trusted friend's kitchen at 6pm, and the production design of a queer indie film. Not minimalist in the Apple-white sense — warmer than that, with real texture. Closer in spirit to Lex's intentional text-first queer aesthetic and Feeld's modern-romantic restraint, but softer, more approachable, and centered in trans femininity without being pink-princess. If the product feels like it was designed by a trans woman's art-direction friend for other trans women, the mood is right.

### Color Palette

The palette is warm, rosy, and intentionally adjacent to (but not derivative of) the trans flag. Plum is the primary dark; warm cream is the primary light; rose and peach are the accents; sage green is the support color for success states.

- `--color-primary` / `plum-600` — `#6B2E4F` — primary CTAs, headers, brand surfaces.
- `--color-primary-hover` / `plum-700` — `#4F1D3A` — hover/pressed states on plum.
- `--color-primary-tint` / `plum-50` — `#F7EDF3` — subtle backgrounds, badges.

**Secondary — Rose:**
- `--color-secondary` / `rose-500` — `#E8859A` — secondary accents, icons, highlights.
- `--color-secondary-soft` / `rose-200` — `#F7D4DC` — soft fills, hover tints.

**Accent — Peach:**
- `--color-accent` / `peach-400` — `#F4B789` — micro-highlights, illustrations, celebratory moments.

**Support — Sage:**
- `--color-support` / `sage-500` — `#8BA888` — success states, verified badges.

**Background:**

- `--color-surface` / `cream-100` — `#F5EBDC` — card surfaces.

Contrast ratios: all text-on-background pairs meet WCAG AA at 4.5:1 minimum. Primary plum on cream clears 9:1.

### Typography

**Heading font:** *Fraunces* (Google Fonts, free). A warm serif with soft, humanist letterforms — romantic without being precious. Weights to load: 400, 500, 600, 700. Used for all headings, hero copy, and profile names.

**Body font:** *Inter* (Google Fonts, free). Clean, friendly sans-serif; handles small sizes and long reading well. Weights to load: 400, 500, 600. Used for body, UI labels, prompts, and messages.

**Mono font:** *JetBrains Mono* (Google Fonts, free). Used only for developer-facing surfaces (admin dashboards, debug).

**Type scale (rem, base 16px):**
- `--text-xs` 0.75rem (12px) — metadata, timestamps.
- `--text-sm` 0.875rem (14px) — secondary UI.
- `--text-base` 1rem (16px) — body.
- `--text-lg` 1.125rem (18px) — emphasized body.
- `--text-xl` 1.25rem (20px) — small headings, prompt questions.
- `--text-2xl` 1.5rem (24px) — profile names, section headers.
- `--text-3xl` 2rem (32px) — screen titles.
- `--text-4xl` 2.5rem (40px) — hero headlines.
- `--text-display` 3rem (48px) — splash / onboarding moments.

**Line heights:** headings 1.2; body 1.5; prompts 1.4.

### Spacing & Layout

4px base unit. Scale: `--space-1` 4px, `--space-2` 8px, `--space-3` 12px, `--space-4` 16px, `--space-6` 24px, `--space-8` 32px, `--space-12` 48px, `--space-16` 64px, `--space-24` 96px.

**Rules:**
- Minimum 24px between major profile sections (photos, prompts, identity block).
- Minimum 16px touch target padding on all tappable elements (iOS/Android HIG).
- Maximum content width for reading contexts (prompts, bios) is 640px.
- Profile card edges never go flush to screen edges; minimum 16px page gutter on mobile.
- Safe area insets respected on all surfaces (no content under notches or home indicators).

**Breakpoints (for future web companion):** 480 / 768 / 1024 / 1280.

### Component Philosophy

**Border radius:** 12px default for cards and inputs; 8px for buttons; 24px for rounded images and avatar frames; fully rounded (9999px) for pill elements (intention tags, identity chips).

**Shadows:** minimal and warm. One standard elevation: `0 2px 8px rgba(107, 46, 79, 0.08)` for cards; `0 8px 24px rgba(107, 46, 79, 0.12)` for modals. No hard black shadows anywhere.

**Borders:** avoid where possible. When needed, `1px solid rgba(107, 46, 79, 0.08)` — a soft plum hairline, not a gray box.

**Buttons:** primary is filled plum; secondary is outlined plum on cream; tertiary is text-only plum. Pressed state scales down 2% with a subtle haptic on mobile. Destructive actions (block, report, delete) use rose-700.

**Inputs:** oversized touch targets (48px minimum height). Labels sit above, not inside, the input. Placeholder text is warm and specific ("Tell people what makes you laugh" — not "Enter bio").

**Cards:** generous padding (24px minimum). Soft background (cream-100). No hard borders.

### Iconography & Imagery

**Icons:** Phosphor Icons (free, consistent across platforms). Use the regular (outline) weight as default, filled weight for active/selected states. Never mix weights on the same screen.

**Illustrations:** hand-drawn-feeling line art with warm fills, in the Phosphor-Lucide family aesthetic. Commissioned where budget allows. No generic vector stock art.

**Photography:** real photography only. For marketing: commissioned shoots of real trans community members with consent, compensation, and final approval. Natural light, imperfect, at-home feeling. No stock. No fashion-shoot aesthetic. No body-focused angles.

**User-uploaded photos:** presented with generous radii and soft frames. Never cropped into hard rectangles. Aspect ratios preserved where possible; no aggressive auto-crop that could cut off hair, jewelry, or makeup mid-face.

**What to avoid:** stock photo models, corporate "diversity" imagery, fashion-shoot body poses, hands-holding-phone-in-café shots, rainbow-filtered everything, any image that could appear on a consumer bank's homepage.

### Accessibility Commitments

- WCAG 2.1 AA compliance at launch; AAA on type contrast.
- Minimum touch target: 44×44 points (iOS HIG baseline).
- Text resizing: supports up to 200% OS text scale without layout breakage.
- Screen reader: all interactive elements have accessible labels. Profile photos have user-provided alt text (optional).
- Focus indicators: visible on every interactive element, using a 2px plum ring at 2px offset.
- Color contrast: 4.5:1 minimum for body text, 3:1 for large text, 3:1 for UI components.
- Captions / transcripts: all audio prompts are auto-transcribed; transcripts shown inline.
- Motion: respects `prefers-reduced-motion` — when enabled, transitions drop to 100ms linear fades; no bouncy animations.
- Dynamic Type: iOS Dynamic Type and Android font-scaling respected throughout.

### Motion & Interaction

**Transition durations:** 150ms for micro (button press, chip toggle), 250ms for UI (modal open, screen transition), 400ms for hero (magic moments, match celebrations). Ease-out curve by default.

**What animates:** screen transitions (slide + fade), modals (scale from 0.96 with fade), the match-made celebration (gentle confetti + haptic, under 1.2s total), pull-to-refresh (custom plum spinner).

**What does NOT animate:** profile content when scrolling (no parallax), prompt answers (static), identity fields (static).

**Loading states:** skeleton loaders in cream-100 with subtle plum shimmer. Never spinners for content — spinners only for system actions under 2 seconds.

**Haptics (mobile):** light tap on button press, light success on match, medium success on verified completion, light warning on report submission. Respect system haptic settings.
